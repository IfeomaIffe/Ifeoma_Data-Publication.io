Research Goals

I am proposing for a mixed method computational algorithm of image and text retrieval pulled from American historical newspapers to present newspapers in circulation today. Specifically, to create an image detection algorithm focused on getting better recognition of persons with darker skin complexions. To ground this research, I plan on pulling methods and theories from a multitude of areas of study and will go through a short review of a few main ones to establish some context. I will conclude with evaluating how the algorithms pertain to my research goals.

Research Questions

Who are the most represented, and what does this say? Who are represented as insiders/outsiders and how has this changed with time? What do these photos show about American journalism, and how are they reflective of the visual storytelling of the present day? Do graphic images have the ability to alter public support for or against U.S. police systems and civil rights issues? Are we statistically telling and retelling the same stories? Are these the stories we want to tell?

Methods and Literature Review

The project idea originating from the Newspaper Navigator dataset released by the Library of Congress, which has over 1.56 million visuals collected from over 16 million historic newspaper pages from 1850 - 1963. This dataset has been made open access, along with pre-packaged datasets and the corresponding code available for public use. The datasets organized by year include images, cartoons, advertisements, and other visual content types. In addition, including textual content a headline; likewise, for a photograph, illustration, or map, this textual representation often contains the title and caption, Place of Publication, Geographic Coverage, Newspaper Name, Newspaper Publisher, and other metadata fields. However, because it is lacking in an adequate representation, its complete use for my study is no longer an option. This problem is addressed by its creator Benjamin Lee in his findings,

While this loss in image quality is marginalizing in its own right, image embeddings perpetuate this marginalization: digitized newspaper portraits of darker-skinned individuals are more likely to suffer from saturated facial features, in turn resulting in these photographs being lost during the discovery and retrieval process… Though machine learning methods are often offered as panaceas for automation, this algorithmic erasure reminds us that traditional methods of scholarship and historiography, such as detailed analyses and close readings of Black newspapers in Chronicling America, are more important than ever to counter algorithmic bias (Lee, 2020, p.28).

Deriving from this project, I am proposing to create my own of database from first the Library of Congress API, in addition to many other APIs to locate images pulled from all newspapers, but with the specific concentration on newspapers from racialized populations, where there remains a difficulty of image detection precision. The datasets will be constructed this way, one of mainly white newspapers, and one oia collection of newspapers from various subcultures of racialized Americans from the 1880s to present, where sets will be grouped by every 20 years.

First, I will need to do further research into language creation of race and formulate an ethical codifying system. As stated by Martin Berger, in the introduction to his book, Seeing Through Race, “In order to conduct a computation analysis of a large, digitized corpus of photographic materials, it is necessary to develop a code system for describing each image” (2011, p.11). I am thinking I may also need to construct a timeline featuring all the occurrences of civil unrest in American history, in addition to a taxonomy for mapping relationships between words, and their connotations along with metadata tags. This could be transferred to an ontology to organize how words have changed over time, also to describe the data models and map how they are to be used. I believe this is help simplify and organize the compounding computational theories and methods.

Subsequently, I will need to conduct several rounds of web scrapping to build an alternative dataset, apart from the APIS, of present-day newspaper images. With this, I am thinking of expanding upon the textual data that is currently not available in the Newspaper Navigator datasets, also whether I should include digital images from newspaper/media websites. Once a sizable corpus has been collected, I plan to use data management tools such as OpenRefine, to do clean up and Libre Office quickly review data and run smaller tests of various image recognition models, while I continue building the corpus.

Once the corpus has been complied, a computational image detection algorithm(s) has been finalized, and the data analysis model has proved promising, I will then use MySQL, to run more detailed queries on all the data. The next step would be to run iterations of the imaging programs and use Jupyter Notebook to analyze the statistical outputs, and run a multivariate regression or Principle Component Analysis, in addition to using it to create data visualizations. There are many algorithmic options for image detection, some focusing on facial features, or the whole images, or for parsing out elements within the image, and others for evaluating artistic content and style.

The first is being Computational Grounded Theory (CGT) which is relatively new. As proposed by researcher Laura K. Nelson, takes into account aspects from both traditional Content Analysis (CA) and Computer-assisted Content Analysis (CCA) mixed with Grounded theory. “Grounded theory is a method designed to allow categories and themes to emerge inductively from data, culminating in data-driven but abstract theoretical understandings of the underlying social world” (p.5). The traditional qualitative practice of CA uses coding – this process is clearly construed by Nelson,

[As a way to] increase accuracy, multiple coders will code the same text and their agreement on codes is checked via an intercoder reliability score. When the text is coded with a reasonably high intercoder reliability (or, sometimes, by one expert researcher), the researcher then analyzes the coded text, looking for different types of patterns within and between texts (2020, p.6)

Attempting to interpret the text holistically is a difficult task, because according to Nelson there is no way to make linguistic coding scientific. According to Nelson, the process itself obscures rather than reveals what the researcher is trying to say because it is relatively experimental in nature, and in many cases completely unreproducible (p.7). Nelson proposes CCA will make up for the limitations of content analysis, by using computational techniques like pattern detection and pattern refinement with Guided Deep Reading to crate lexical selection and topic modeling (p.14). I believe the CGT method would be well suited for the textual content collected.

As for image retrieval and classification, Distant Viewing, is “a methodological and theoretical framework for studying a large collection of visual materials, by making the interpretive nature of extracting semantic metadata from images more present” (Arnold et al., 2019, p.11). As described by the researchers, the first step to distant viewing is constructing, “… a representation of elements contained within the visual material-a code system in semiotics or, similarly, a metadata schema in informatics” (p.11). What they call computer vision is then used to explore photography without human intervention in how it is interpreted, or how the information is created and collected. The algorithm – YOLOv4 – is a recognition system used that can in real-time identify multiple objects from an image and apply boundary boxes around them. Additionally, it can be easily trained and used, which makes it appealing option.

Another method, Scale Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT) is another detection tool to find, describe and match local features in directly compressed images even if images have different rotations and scales. Researchers Klein and Shapira, in Compressed SIFT Feature-based Matching, describes the process where the algorithm first constructs a “scale space to ensure scale invariance”, then it repeatedly blurs out the original image until it decreases to half its size (p.65). These blurred images then generate additional sets of images. Afterwards, Laplacian of Gaussian (LoG) operation is applied, and its process is described by the researchers,

The LoG operation locates edges and corners in the image, which are used for finding keypoints. However, since calculating the LoG is computationally intensive, it is approximated by the Difference of Gaussians (DoG), calculating the difference between two consecutive scales, resulting in scale invariant keypoints… Maxima and minima pixels are chosen as keypoints, which cannot be detected in the lowest or highest scales. Edges and low contrast pixels are eliminated from the set of keypoints. An orientation is calculated for each keypoint, choosing the most dominant one(s) around the keypoint… Highly distinctive vectors are then created for each keypoint as follows.

This method piques my interest for its implemented could drastically decrease the storage necessary for housing this data. This algorithm is rather complex, and I will continue researching it and how it works in multiple contexts.

Finally, there are Convolutional Neural Network and Fully Convolutional Networks (FCN) algorithms that outputs a pixel-labeling of the various semantic entities. In Sollard et al., study, Multi-scale Gated Fully Convolutional DenseNets for semantic labeling of historical newspaper images, they use FCN in conjunction with Multi-Scale Gated Block architecture (MSGB), to make dense connections and gating mechanisms which can handle a multi-scale analysis of inserted image with “self-attention" (2020, p.437.) Though this approach is used for binarized images, but I believe there are aspects of the framework from which I can borrow from for my study.

If things go accordingly, an experimental idea I have is to implement a CNN, such as ResNet50 and VGG16 (Pillai, 2020) to extract features from images then combine those that have similarities from different times in American history that have aligned artistic qualities (e. g. content, style, framing, movement of objects). A study by Gatys et al., uses a CNN to reconstruct content and style of two different artists from different mediums into one final product.

When Convolutional Neural Networks are trained on object recognition, they develop a representation of the image that makes object information increasingly explicit along the processing hierarchy.8 Therefore, along the processing hierarchy of the network, the input image is transformed into representations that increasingly care about the actual content of the image compared to its detailed pixel values. We can directly visualize the information each layer contains about the input image by reconstructing the image only from the feature maps in that layer (2016, p.6).

I believe with a CNN there is a way to take a photograph from 1949 looking just like an image from 1959 and another from 1988 and mashed together and shown as one but viewers can tell that they were once individual images. The idea being combining pixels proportionally to show a new image, combined with showing enough of the others and how they relate and come together. Personally, I have always been a visual learner, and I believe this could be a new additional visualization tool, that would be pair along with other statistic visuals (e.g., charts, scatter plots, tables).

There are parts from this study that apply to my own, however, if combine with matching algorithmic aspects from the SIFT method, I believe this could be feasible. Once, the computational algorithms have been established, and tests have been run, I will need to review my results in context of what I have found from what other qualitative scholars have been findings. Literature Review

Beginning with Visual Semiotics and Critical Discourse Analysis which are two theories address linguistic ways relations of power, dominance and inequality is reproduced and influence the minds and actions of subconsciously (Van Dijk, 1995). One of the problems with theories such as these is that they are seen to be unscientific and biased. However, this is only the case because discourse is inherently political. The CDA emphasizes the conclusions, must work, prove something, and be reproducible. Semiotics, the study of signs and symbols is a philosophy to the mechanisms of creating these symbols within culture(s). It is culture that becomes the primary force, and according to Stuart Hall, a renown cultural theorist, “Cultures consist of the maps of meaning, the frameworks of intelligibility, the things which allow us to make sense of a world which exists but is ambiguous as to its meaning until we’ve made sense of it” (2002, p.9). Language and knowledge creation are relational, and can never be finally fixed, but is subject to the constant process of redefinition and appropriation.

Researcher Clément’s (2016) classifies three ways in which information operates. For my proposed project falls within two of the three categories of information sharing, information-as-knowledge, and information-as-process.

Information as knowledge is intangible since it is based on personal, subjective and conceptual understandings… information as process is the systems of powers and influence that shape information systems and therefore knowledge production, identity construction, and intersubjectivity Information- as- process — the continual state of becoming informed through understandings that are constantly shifting (pp 154-156).

Newspapers, like history textbooks, television programs, Hollywood movies, to music and other external ways of expression was always under constraint where certain false ideas, stories, and perceptions are pushed and peddled by control agents. In most cases, these falsehoods work against disempowered communities. Texts of all forms have controlled processes in creation, dissemination, and authorization of knowledge. Researchers Cope al et., captures this point in their article looking at the impact of newspaper images.

Images of combat operations, both positive and negative, can take on an iconic status with the American public... One possible solution to combat the powerful influence of photos is through government censorship... Although many people commonly accept the premise of news images’ ability to sway public opinion, very little research has been done to test this assumption. Does the use of images with text affect readers? (2005, pp. 3-4).

This study looks at the nature of how pictorial stimuli tend to get more attention from people than the articles and how they can be used effectively to catch readers attention combined with only a few words. Newspaper photos are utilized in many ways, but in the case of wartime imagery are used to convey specific experiences that most will never directly encounter and increase emotional involvement.

Photographs in news stories bring readers directly to the front of the action and offer a front-row seat for U.S. combat operations. Reading about a particular battle is informative but viewing graphic images of war dead from that battle allows the news consumer to feel drawn into the action...Photos have the ability to convey drama and emotion and realism in a way that text alone cannot. Visual images can short circuit higher levels of cognition and reasoning because they are more lifelike and easier for the brain to process (Cope al et., pg. 8).

To add another study looking at similar effects in photojournalism, researchers Fishman and Marvin in their study Portrayals of Violence and Group Difference in Newspaper Photographs: Nationalism in Media (2003). Looking at only The New York Times, their results find that images of non-U.S. violence is almost always has a more severe representation depicted than of U.S. violence, and is a commonly used feature for the front-page. Even with non-U.S. agents having a smaller sample, were still associated more often with explicit violence. 3.7 times more numerous than those of U.S. agents (p. 26). Though I will only be looking at domestic conflicts, these last two studies show the implicit and explicit biases within published images distort Americans emotions and perceptions of violence.
